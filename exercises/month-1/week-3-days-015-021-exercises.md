## Day 15-16: Word Stress + Sentence Stress

### 📝 Exercises

**Ex 1: Đánh dấu trọng âm (gạch chân âm nhấn)**
1. de-VE-lop-er ✓ hay DE-ve-lop-er ✗?
2. ap-pli-CA-tion ✓ hay AP-pli-ca-tion ✗?
3. en-VI-ron-ment ✓ hay en-vi-RON-ment ✗?
4. au-then-ti-CA-tion ✓ hay au-THEN-ti-ca-tion ✗?
5. PER-for-mance ✓ hay per-FOR-mance ✓? → per-FOR-mance

**Ex 2: Sentence stress — Nhấn content words**
Gạch chân từ cần nhấn:
1. "I need to fix the bug in the code." → **NEED, FIX, BUG, CODE**
2. "The server crashed after the deployment." → **SERVER, CRASHED, DEPLOYMENT**
3. "We should test the feature before release." → **SHOULD, TEST, FEATURE, RELEASE**

**Ex 3: Intonation — Rising ↗️ hay Falling ↘️?**
1. "Where is the bug?" → ↘️ (Wh-question)
2. "Is the server running?" → ↗️ (Yes/No)
3. "I deployed the app." → ↘️ (Statement)
4. "Did you push the code?" → ↗️ (Yes/No)
5. "What's the error message?" → ↘️ (Wh-question)

### 🎧 Listening Text

```
Let me talk about how stress and intonation work in English.

English is a stress-timed language. This means the important words receive more emphasis, while less important words are spoken quickly and softly. In the sentence "I NEED to FIX the BUG," the words "need," "fix," and "bug" are stressed because they carry the main meaning. The words "I," "to," and "the" are spoken quickly.

Word stress is also important. Every word with more than one syllable has one syllable that is louder, longer, and higher in pitch. For example, in "developer," the stress is on the second syllable: de-VE-lo-per. If you say DE-ve-lo-per, native speakers might not understand you.

There are some useful rules. Most two-syllable nouns stress the first syllable: PRO-gram, SYS-tem, SER-ver. Most two-syllable verbs stress the second syllable: de-PLOY, de-BUG, in-STALL.

Words ending in "-tion" or "-sion" stress the syllable before the ending: ap-pli-CA-tion, de-CI-sion. Words ending in "-ity" also stress the syllable before: se-CU-ri-ty, a-vai-la-BI-li-ty.

For intonation, use falling tone at the end of statements and wh-questions. Use rising tone for yes-no questions. This helps your English sound natural and confident.
```

## Day 17-18: Connected Speech + Commonly Mispronounced IT Words

### 📝 Exercises

**Ex 1: Linking — Nối đọc**
Đọc to, nối các từ:
1. "check_it" → "chekit"
2. "set_up" → "setup"
3. "turn_it_off" → "turnitoff"
4. "log_in" → "login"
5. "an_error" → "anerror"

**Ex 2: IT Pronunciation Quiz**
Chọn cách đọc đúng:
1. cache: /kæʃ/ hay /kɑːʃeɪ/? → **/kæʃ/** (như "cash")
2. queue: /kjuː/ hay /kwiː/? → **/kjuː/** (như "cue")
3. boolean: /ˈbuːliən/ hay /ˈbuːlin/? → **/ˈbuːliən/**
4. paradigm: /ˈpærədaɪm/ hay /ˈpærədɪɡm/? → **/ˈpærədaɪm/**
5. nginx: /ˌendʒɪnˈeks/ hay /ŋɪnks/? → **/ˌendʒɪnˈeks/**
6. PostgreSQL: /ˈpoʊstɡres/ hay /ˈpɒstɡreɪskjuːɛl/? → **/ˈpoʊstɡres/**
7. Kubernetes: /kuːbərˈnetiːz/ hay /ˈkjuːbəneɪts/? → **/kuːbərˈnetiːz/**

**Ex 3: Schwa /ə/ — Tìm schwa**
Gạch chân âm tiết có schwa:
1. comput**e**r → /kəmˈpjuːt**ə**r/
2. d**a**tabase → /ˈdeɪt**ə**beɪs/
3. **a**bout → /**ə**ˈbaʊt/
4. develop**e**r → /dɪˈvel**ə**p**ə**r/
5. p**a**rameter → /p**ə**ˈræmɪtər/

### 🎧 Listening Text

```
One of the biggest challenges for non-native English speakers is understanding connected speech. When native speakers talk, they don't pronounce each word separately. Instead, words flow together.

For example, "check it out" sounds like "che-ki-tout." "Set it up" sounds like "se-ti-tup." "Turn it off" sounds like "tur-ni-toff." Understanding these patterns is key to improving your listening comprehension.

Native speakers also reduce common words. "Want to" becomes "wanna." "Going to" becomes "gonna." "Have to" becomes "hafta." You don't need to speak this way, but you must understand it when you hear it.

Now, let me correct some commonly mispronounced tech words. Cache is pronounced "cash," not "ca-shay." Queue is "cue," not "kwee." Boolean is "boo-lee-un," not "boo-lin." The word paradigm is "pa-ra-dime," not "pa-ra-dig-um."

For tool names: Kubernetes is "koo-ber-NEH-teez." PostgreSQL is commonly called "Postgres." Nginx is "engine-X." These might seem small, but pronouncing these correctly will make you sound much more professional in technical discussions.

The schwa sound is the most common vowel in English. It appears in unstressed syllables and sounds like a soft "uh." In the word "computer," the first syllable has a schwa: "kuh-PYOO-ter." In "database," the second syllable: "DAY-tuh-base." Learning to recognize the schwa will help both your listening and pronunciation.
```

- Số liệu (users, response time, uptime...)
- 1 thử thách và cách giải quyết

### 🎧 Listening Text — Day 21 Review

```
This week we learned about suprasegmental features — the patterns above individual sounds that make English sound natural.

Word stress tells us which syllable to emphasize. Remember: two-syllable nouns usually stress syllable one, like "PRO-gram" and "SER-ver." Two-syllable verbs usually stress syllable two, like "de-PLOY" and "de-BUG." Words ending in "tion" stress the syllable before: "ap-pli-CA-tion."

Sentence stress means we emphasize content words — nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs — while saying function words quickly. "I NEED to FIX the BUG" has three stressed words.

Intonation gives our sentences melody. We use falling intonation for statements and wh-questions: "The server is running." "Where is the bug?" We use rising intonation for yes-no questions: "Is the server running?"

Connected speech explains why native speakers seem to speak so fast. Words link together: "check it" becomes "chekit." Words get reduced: "going to" becomes "gonna."

Finally, we corrected the pronunciation of common IT terms. Remember: cache is "cash," queue is "cue," boolean is "boo-lee-un," and Kubernetes is "koo-ber-NEH-teez."

If you can master these patterns, your English will sound much more natural and professional. Keep practicing every day!
```
